# Circadian Basics

A short primer on the body clock and what jetlag actually is.

## What the body clock is

Your body runs on an internal clock that keeps a roughly 24-hour rhythm even with no
outside cues. This rhythm governs when you feel sleepy, when you feel alert, your core
body temperature, and the release of hormones like melatonin and cortisol. Left alone in
constant darkness, the human clock tends to drift a little longer than 24 hours, which is
why daily light cues are needed to keep it anchored to the real day.

## The circadian rhythm and the SCN

The master clock lives in a small cluster of cells in the brain called the
suprachiasmatic nucleus, or SCN. The SCN takes its strongest signal from light reaching
the eyes and uses it to keep your internal day lined up with the external day. It then
coordinates clocks throughout the rest of the body, from the liver to the gut, so they
all run on the same schedule.

## What jetlag actually is

Jetlag is the mismatch between your internal clock and local time after you cross several
time zones quickly. Your body still thinks it is on home time while the destination runs
on a different schedule, so you feel wide awake at the wrong hours and exhausted at
others. It is not the same as ordinary travel fatigue; it is specifically a clock-versus-
clock conflict.

## Why the symptoms happen

When your clock and local time disagree, the systems the clock controls fire at the wrong
moments. That produces the classic symptoms: daytime fatigue, mental fog and poor
concentration, broken or shallow night sleep, and digestive upset because your gut clock
is still expecting meals on home time. Mood and appetite often wobble for the same reason.

## The one-hour-per-day rule of thumb

A useful planning rule is that the body clock can only shift about one hour per day on its
own. So crossing six time zones means roughly six days to fully re-sync if you do nothing
to help it along. Good light timing, sleep timing, and meal timing can speed this up, but
the underlying clock still moves gradually rather than all at once.
