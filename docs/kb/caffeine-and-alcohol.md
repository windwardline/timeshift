# Caffeine and Alcohol

How two common travel drinks help or hurt your adjustment.

## Caffeine to fight the afternoon dip

Caffeine can be a useful tool for staying awake until local bedtime, especially through the
mid-afternoon energy dip on arrival day. A cup in the morning or early afternoon can keep
you going so you do not crash-nap. The key is using it deliberately to hold your schedule
rather than drinking it all day.

## Half-life and cut-off times

Caffeine has a long half-life — often around five to six hours — meaning a meaningful
amount is still in your system hours after you drink it. To protect night sleep, stop
caffeine by the early afternoon on local time, roughly six to eight hours before your
target bedtime. An espresso after dinner can quietly wreck the night you are trying to
build.

## Why alcohol fragments sleep

Alcohol may make you feel drowsy at first, but it fragments sleep later in the night as it
clears, producing shallow, broken rest. On top of an already-confused clock, that broken
sleep makes jetlag worse rather than better. A nightcap to "help you sleep" on arrival
usually backfires.

## Hydration on long-haul

Cabin air is very dry and long flights make dehydration easy, which amplifies fatigue and
headaches that feel like jetlag. Drinking water steadily through the flight, and going easy
on both caffeine and alcohol aloft, helps you arrive feeling closer to normal. Hydration
will not move your clock, but it keeps the avoidable symptoms down.
