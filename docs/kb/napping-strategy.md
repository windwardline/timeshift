# Napping Strategy

How to nap during jetlag recovery without sabotaging night sleep.

## Strategic short naps

A strategic nap is short — roughly 20 to 30 minutes — and timed to restore alertness
without pulling you into deep sleep. Kept brief, it tops up your energy and leaves the
night's sleep largely intact. This is the only kind of nap that reliably helps during
jetlag recovery.

## Why long naps backfire

A long nap of an hour or more lets you fall into deep sleep and burns off the sleep
pressure your body needs to fall asleep that night. Wake from a long afternoon nap and you
may find yourself staring at the ceiling at 2 a.m., which sets your adjustment back. The
longer and later the nap, the more it works against you.

## Nap timing windows

The best window for a recovery nap is usually early in the local afternoon, well before
evening. Napping too close to local bedtime steals from your night, while a short
early-afternoon nap aligns with the body's natural mid-day dip. Keep naps in that early
window and set an alarm.

## Sleep pressure versus circadian drive

Two forces govern sleepiness: sleep pressure, which builds the longer you stay awake, and
the circadian drive, which depends on the time on your body clock. Napping releases sleep
pressure, so a well-timed short nap eases daytime fatigue while leaving enough pressure to
sleep at night. A long nap dumps too much of that pressure at once.

## When not to nap

If you arrive in the evening or are close to local bedtime, skip the nap entirely and go to
bed on the local schedule. Napping also is not worth it when you are only mildly tired and
can make it to bedtime on your own. The goal is always to protect that first solid night of
local-time sleep.
