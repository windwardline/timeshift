# The Master Clock (SCN)

How a tiny cluster of brain cells keeps your whole body on the same schedule.

## Where the clock lives

The master clock sits in a small group of cells in the brain called the suprachiasmatic
nucleus, or SCN. It receives a direct signal from light-sensing cells in the eyes and uses
that signal to keep your internal day lined up with the external day. It is the conductor
that the rest of the body follows.

## Coordinating the body

The SCN does not act alone — clocks exist in the liver, gut, muscles, and other tissues.
The SCN keeps these peripheral clocks synchronized so they all run on the same time. Jetlag
is partly the SCN and these peripheral clocks falling out of step with each other as well as
with local time.

## Why light dominates

Of all the inputs, light reaching the eye is by far the strongest signal the SCN responds
to. That is why correctly timed light is the most powerful tool for shifting the clock, and
why poorly timed light can set your adjustment back.
