# Alcohol and Sleep

Why a nightcap makes jetlag worse, not better.

## The rebound effect

Alcohol may make you drowsy at first, but as it clears it fragments sleep later in the night,
producing shallow, broken rest. On top of an already-confused clock, that broken sleep deepens
jetlag rather than easing it.

## It blunts the clock's cues

Alcohol can also reduce the body's sensitivity to the light-and-dark cues that shift melatonin
and body temperature, making it harder for your clock to realign. So a drink to "help you
sleep" on arrival tends to backfire on two fronts.

## On the plane and after

Cabin air is dry and a long flight already taxes you, so alcohol aloft adds dehydration to
fragmented sleep. Going easy on alcohol both in the air and in the first nights gives your
clock and your sleep a cleaner chance to settle.
