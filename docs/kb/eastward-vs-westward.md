# Eastward vs. Westward Travel

Why the direction you fly changes how hard jetlag hits.

## Why eastward is harder

Flying east forces your clock to move earlier, a shift called a phase advance. You have to
fall asleep and wake up sooner than your body wants, and the human clock resists being
pushed earlier because its natural tendency is to run slightly long. This is why most
travelers find an eastward trip noticeably more punishing than the same distance west.

## Why westward is easier

Flying west asks your clock to move later, a phase delay. Staying up a little longer and
sleeping in lines up with the clock's natural drift toward a longer day, so the adjustment
feels closer to a normal late night. Westward recovery is usually faster and gentler for
the same number of zones crossed.

## East is early, west is late

A simple way to remember the strategy: "east = early, west = late." Heading east, you want
to shift everything earlier — earlier sleep, earlier light, earlier meals. Heading west,
you do the opposite and push everything later. This single rule drives most light and
sleep timing advice.

## Estimating recovery days

A rough estimate for eastward travel is about one day of recovery per time zone crossed.
Westward is often a bit faster, closer to two-thirds of a day per zone. So a six-zone trip
east might take around six days to fully settle, while the same trip west might take four.
These are averages; light and sleep discipline can shorten them.

## Which routes are worst

The hardest routes combine many zones with an eastward direction, such as North America to
Europe or Europe to East Asia. Long eastward red-eyes that land in the morning are
especially tough because you arrive needing to function on a day when your body expects
night. Westward trips of the same length tend to be more forgiving.
